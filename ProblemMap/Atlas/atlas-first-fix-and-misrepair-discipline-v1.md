# Atlas First Fix and Misrepair Discipline 🛠️

## Problem Map 3.0 Troubleshooting Atlas
> Quick links, safe initial repair direction, and beginner-friendly rules for avoiding early misrepair

This page exists because classification alone is not enough.

A system can identify a likely family,  
separate a boundary reasonably well,  
and still fail in practice because the **first repair move** is wrong.

That matters a lot.

Many repair disasters do not happen because the diagnosis was completely random.  
They happen because the first move was too large, too early, too shallow, or aimed at the wrong layer.

This page is here to reduce that risk.

It explains:

- what a first fix is
- what a first fix is not
- what kinds of first moves are usually safer by family
- what kinds of early moves often become misrepair
- how to connect route-first classification to repair-facing work without bluffing full closure

This page is about the first safe move, not the whole war.

## Why this page exists

Atlas is route-first.

That means it helps identify the strongest current structural read before pretending to solve everything at once.

But once a route-first read exists, the next practical question is immediate:

what should I do first?

That question is dangerous.

A bad answer often looks like one of these:

- fix the loudest symptom instead of the broken invariant
- refactor everything before the primary surface is stabilized
- add complexity where constraint is needed
- polish representation while truth remains broken
- add monitoring while the boundary is actively collapsing
- call a deeper rewrite “helpful” when a narrower first move would be safer

So this page exists to make the early move more disciplined.

A first fix should improve the situation without pretending it is already the final repair plan.

## What a first fix is

A first fix is the safest useful **initial repair move** supported by the current structure and evidence.

A good first fix should usually do at least one of these:

- stabilize the primary failure surface
- reduce further drift or damage
- improve the evidence surface for later decisions
- reduce misclassification risk
- create a cleaner base for deeper repair

A first fix is **not** supposed to solve everything.

It is supposed to be:

- early
- justified
- proportionate
- structurally aligned
- less likely to make the problem worse

That is why the phrase “first fix” matters.

It forces discipline.

---

## What a first fix is not

A first fix is not:

### 1. A total rebuild by default
Sometimes the first safe move is small.

### 2. A cosmetic patch that hides the real break
A prettier answer is not always a safer system.

### 3. A full repair plan disguised as a first step
If the move assumes closure you do not yet have, it is already too large.

### 4. A random action taken because something must be done
Urgency is real, but panic is not structure.

### 5. Proof that the diagnosis is fully final
A helpful first move can still coexist with remaining uncertainty.

---

## What misrepair means

Misrepair happens when the chosen repair move makes the system worse, harder to diagnose, more fragile, or more misleading.

Misrepair does not always look dramatic.

Sometimes it looks like:

- a plausible fix that solves the wrong layer
- a local patch that increases downstream confusion
- a large intervention based on an unstable fit
- a metrics improvement that hides deeper failure
- a representation cleanup that makes a false answer look more trustworthy

So misrepair is not only “a totally wrong repair.”

It is also:

a structurally misaligned repair.

---

## Why misrepair happens

Misrepair often happens for very human reasons.

### Reason 1. The loudest symptom steals attention
People repair what is visible first, not what is primary first.

### Reason 2. Action pressure outruns classification quality
People want to move fast even when the fit is still thin.

### Reason 3. Repair vocabulary outruns structure
People know many possible tactics, but not which layer is actually broken.

### Reason 4. Big moves feel more serious
Sometimes the system needs a small stabilizing move, but a big rewrite feels more convincing.

### Reason 5. Surface improvements create false comfort
A cleaner output can hide a deeper unchanged failure.

That is why first-fix discipline matters.

## Family-level first-fix summary table 📚

| Family | Safer first fix direction | Common early misrepair |
|---|---|---|
| F1 | tighten evidence anchoring and source linkage | polish output surface before fixing the anchor |
| F2 | stabilize reasoning progression and missing transitions | add more content without repairing the chain |
| F3 | stabilize continuity, carryover, and state handling | rewrite task logic while continuity is still collapsing |
| F4 | tighten execution path and explicit contract-following | add abstraction while action behavior still fails |
| F5 | improve visibility, tracing, or inspection surfaces | perform large structural repair without enough diagnosis |
| F6 | restore edge discipline and boundary control first | optimize convenience while constraints are still weak |
| F7 | repair packaging, rendering, or localization carefully | assume surface cleanup automatically fixes deeper structure |

## Family-level first-fix guidance

---

## F1 · Grounding and Evidence Integrity 🌍

### Safer first-fix direction
Start by repairing the evidence anchor.

That often means:
- tighten source selection
- tighten retrieval-to-claim linkage
- reduce unsupported answer expansion
- verify whether the current answer is actually tied to the relevant evidence surface

### Why this is the safer first move
If the anchor is broken, many later improvements become misleading.

A better-looking answer built on a false or weak anchor is not a true improvement.

### Common misrepair
The classic F1 misrepair is:
- improving style, structure, or polish before fixing evidential truth linkage

### Why that misrepair is tempting
Because the output often looks obviously bad, and surface cleanup is easy to see.

### Why it is dangerous
It can make a weakly grounded answer look more trustworthy.

---

## F2 · Reasoning and Progression Integrity 🧠

### Safer first-fix direction
Start by repairing the step-to-step progression.

That often means:
- expose missing transitions
- reduce inference jumps
- simplify the reasoning path
- make the chain inspectable before making it longer

### Why this is the safer first move
A broken chain does not become valid by becoming longer, louder, or more detailed.

### Common misrepair
The classic F2 misrepair is:
- adding more content, more steps, or more rhetoric without repairing structural transitions

### Why that misrepair is tempting
Because “more explanation” feels like “better reasoning.”

### Why it is dangerous
It can bury the weak jump under more text instead of fixing it.

---

## F3 · State and Continuity Integrity 🧭

### Safer first-fix direction
Start by stabilizing continuity and carryover.

That often means:
- preserve relevant state explicitly
- reduce uncontrolled context drift
- make continuity surfaces easier to inspect
- simplify the evolving task path before expanding it

### Why this is the safer first move
If the system cannot preserve the thread, deeper logic work may not hold long enough to matter.

### Common misrepair
The classic F3 misrepair is:
- redesigning local reasoning or output style while continuity collapse is still the primary issue

### Why that misrepair is tempting
Because the final answer often looks incoherent, so the repairer blames local intelligence first.

### Why it is dangerous
It leaves the continuity break alive, so the improved local logic still falls apart later.

---

## F4 · Execution and Contract Integrity ⚙️

### Safer first-fix direction
Start by tightening the task contract and action path.

That often means:
- make the explicit contract clearer
- reduce ambiguity in required action
- check whether tool use or action order matches intent
- stabilize the execution surface before adding more abstraction

### Why this is the safer first move
If intention is not reaching action cleanly, adding more interpretation layers can increase noise.

### Common misrepair
The classic F4 misrepair is:
- adding strategy, planning language, or extra architecture while basic contract-following is still broken

### Why that misrepair is tempting
Because it feels more sophisticated to redesign the whole system than to fix a basic action contract.

### Why it is dangerous
It adds complexity before the system can even perform the existing task reliably.

---

## F5 · Observability and Diagnosability Integrity 🔍

### Safer first-fix direction
Start by improving the visibility surface.

That often means:
- expose traces
- improve metrics or diagnostics
- make intermediate states more inspectable
- increase the quality of evidence before making large structural repairs

### Why this is the safer first move
If you cannot see what is really failing, a large repair move becomes guesswork.

### Common misrepair
The classic F5 misrepair is:
- performing heavy structural changes before the system is diagnosable enough

### Why that misrepair is tempting
Because “doing something big” feels more productive than improving visibility.

### Why it is dangerous
It can erase clues, multiply variables, and make the real failure even harder to isolate.

---

## F6 · Boundary and Safety Integrity 🛡️

### Safer first-fix direction
Start by restoring edge discipline.

That often means:
- tighten constraints
- reduce unsafe freedom at the edge
- stabilize refusal or restriction behavior
- make the boundary bite before optimizing anything else

### Why this is the safer first move
A system with weak limit discipline can create damage faster than a system that is merely awkward or incomplete.

### Common misrepair
The classic F6 misrepair is:
- optimizing convenience, usability, or speed while edge control is still weak

### Why that misrepair is tempting
Because stronger boundaries can feel restrictive, slower, or less impressive in demos.

### Why it is dangerous
It prioritizes comfort over control while the most dangerous failure surface remains open.

---

## F7 · Representation and Localization Integrity 📝

### Safer first-fix direction
Start by improving the representation surface carefully.

That often means:
- repair formatting
- repair packaging
- repair localization
- separate representational cleanup from deeper truth claims

### Why this is the safer first move
If the signal is broadly present but badly rendered, cleaner representation can materially improve usability.

### Common misrepair
The classic F7 misrepair is:
- assuming that surface cleanup proves the deeper structure is already fine

### Why that misrepair is tempting
Because the visible improvement is immediate.

### Why it is dangerous
It can hide unresolved F1 or F2 issues under a cleaner representational layer.

---

## Misrepair pattern table

| Misrepair pattern | What it usually does wrong | Common family zones |
|---|---|---|
| Symptom-first patch | repairs the loudest symptom, not the broken invariant | all families |
| Cosmetic rescue | improves surface appearance while deeper structure stays broken | F1, F2, F7 |
| Observability bypass | acts heavily before evidence quality is good enough | F5, F4, F6 |
| Continuity denial | blames local logic while the state surface is collapsing | F3, F2 |
| Boundary underreaction | keeps optimizing while constraints still fail | F6, F4 |
| Architecture inflation | adds complexity before the current layer is stabilized | all families |

## The safest first-fix question

Before choosing a first move, ask this:

> what is the smallest structurally aligned intervention that reduces the main risk without pretending the whole diagnosis is already closed?

That question helps a lot.

It blocks:
- ego repairs
- panic repairs
- theater repairs
- overgrown redesigns

It pushes the system back toward disciplined action.

---

## First-fix restraint rules

A good first fix should respect these rules.

### Rule 1. Match the move to the strongest current fit
Do not use a family-4 fix posture if the case is still mostly family-3 boundary-live.

### Rule 2. Prefer stabilization over expansion
The first move should usually reduce drift, not create more moving parts.

### Rule 3. Prefer visibility before blind large intervention
If the evidence surface is weak, improve diagnosis before betting big.

### Rule 4. Do not let polish impersonate truth
A cleaner answer can still be wrong.

### Rule 5. Keep the move proportionate to the evidence
Weak evidence supports narrower action.

---

## When to stop at a first fix

Sometimes the first fix is enough for now.

That is especially true when:

- the primary failure surface is clear
- the move reduces the main risk cleanly
- deeper action would need stronger evidence first
- the system becomes more diagnosable after the first move

Stopping after a good first fix is not laziness.

It is discipline.

---

## When not to stop at a first fix

Sometimes the first fix only creates a stable base.

Do not pretend it solved the whole issue when:

- boundary ambiguity still remains high
- a subtree-level distinction still matters
- the problem mixes several active surfaces
- the first move was only diagnostic preparation
- the visible improvement is still fragile

In those cases, the first fix is a beginning, not a finish.

---

## Common first-fix mistakes

### Mistake 1. Choosing the largest move
Big moves feel serious, but often carry more misrepair risk.

### Mistake 2. Confusing speed with correctness
A fast move is not always a good first move.

### Mistake 3. Over-trusting a thin fit
If the fit is still weak, the repair move should stay narrow.

### Mistake 4. Letting prettier output end the investigation
A prettier surface can cause premature closure.

### Mistake 5. Fixing what is easiest to edit
Ease of intervention is not the same as structural priority.

## Example first-fix wording patterns

These are not full output contracts.  
They are example language patterns for disciplined early repair posture.

### Example 1. Clear family-aligned first fix
> current primary fit: F1 at family level  
> safest first fix: tighten evidence anchoring and reduce unsupported answer expansion before touching presentation quality

### Example 2. Boundary-live with narrow first move
> current read remains boundary-live between F3 and F4  
> safest first move: stabilize continuity trace and execution contract visibility before larger intervention

### Example 3. Under-evidenced case
> fit remains under-evidenced at the F5/F6 boundary  
> safest first move: improve observability and confirm whether the primary failure is diagnostic blindness or edge-control failure

### Example 4. Representation with restraint
> current primary fit: F7  
> safest first fix: clean the representation surface, but do not treat that as proof that deeper grounding and reasoning layers are already healthy

## Practical use

Here is the simplest practical workflow.

### Step 1
Identify the strongest current structural fit:
- [Atlas Family Mini-Specs](./atlas-family-mini-specs-v1.md)
- [Atlas Boundary Decision Guide](./atlas-boundary-decision-guide-v1.md)
- [Atlas Fit Candidate Registry](./atlas-fit-candidate-registry-v1.md)

### Step 2
Ask:
- what is broken first
- what is the smallest aligned move
- what move would most likely make this worse if I guessed wrong

### Step 3
Choose the narrowest justified first move.

### Step 4
Check whether the move:
- reduces drift
- improves evidence
- lowers risk
- avoids misrepair theater

### Step 5
Only then decide whether deeper repair is justified.

That sequence keeps Atlas useful in practice.
