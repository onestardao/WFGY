## Problem Map 3.0 Troubleshooting Atlas
> Quick links, seven-family mini-specs, and beginner-friendly route-first interpretation

This page exists for a very practical reason.

Many readers can already tell that the Atlas uses a seven-family structure.  
What they still often need is a clean page that says:

- what each family roughly means
- what kind of failure surface it points to
- what kind of broken invariant usually matters most
- what it should not be confused with
- when a family-level reading is enough, and when it is not

This page gives that first clean layer.

It does **not** try to settle every boundary case.  
It does **not** try to expose every subtree.  
It does **not** try to replace the full freeze documents.

Its job is simpler and more useful:

help readers understand the seven families in a stable, readable, route-first form.

## Why this page exists

The Atlas already has a stronger and deeper core body than a short summary page can hold.

That is good for rigor, but it also creates a normal readability problem:

new readers, collaborators, and even strong AI systems may know that there are seven families, while still lacking a clean family-level description they can hold in working memory.

Without that middle layer, people often do one of two wrong things:

- they stay too vague and only repeat the family names
- they jump too fast into detailed routing, subtree, or repair claims

This page avoids both problems.

It gives a compact but structured answer to the family question first.

That makes later documents easier to read.

## How to read family-level classification

Family-level classification is useful, but it has to be read with discipline.

A family in Atlas is **not** just a topic label.  
It is a route-first structural grouping that points toward the most meaningful failure surface.

That means a family answer should help you ask:

- what seems broken first
- what invariant is not holding
- what kind of evidence would strengthen or weaken this placement
- what kind of first repair direction becomes plausible
- what neighboring family might be confused with it

So the safest way to read this page is:

family first,  
boundary second,  
subtree later,  
fit and repair after that.

That order keeps the system clear.

## Seven-family summary table 📚

| Family | Main intuition | Usual broken invariant | Common beginner confusion |
|---|---|---|---|
| F1 | The system is not anchored to the right evidence or grounding surface | grounding or evidence integrity | confused with representation or observability problems |
| F2 | The reasoning path does not progress in a coherent or valid way | reasoning or progression integrity | confused with state continuity or representation problems |
| F3 | The system loses continuity across steps, memory, turns, or evolving state | state or continuity integrity | confused with execution or reasoning failures |
| F4 | The system cannot reliably carry intention into action, execution, or contract-following behavior | execution or contract integrity | confused with observability or safety boundary problems |
| F5 | The system cannot be read, diagnosed, measured, or inspected clearly enough | observability or diagnosability integrity | confused with safety control or grounding failures |
| F6 | The system crosses limits badly, lacks constraint discipline, or mishandles boundaries | boundary or safety integrity | confused with observability or execution failures |
| F7 | The system represents, packages, localizes, or renders meaning badly even when deeper intent may partly exist | representation or localization integrity | confused with grounding or reasoning failures |

## The seven family mini-specs

---

## F1 · Grounding and Evidence Integrity 🌍

### Core intuition
F1 cases are about the system not being properly tied to the right evidence surface, source surface, or grounding surface.

The system may appear fluent.  
It may even sound structurally confident.  
But the underlying anchor is weak, missing, mismatched, or not actually connected to the claim.

### What usually breaks first
The first thing that breaks is usually the connection between claim and support.

That support might involve:

- retrieval evidence
- source selection
- citation anchor
- document alignment
- observation-to-claim linkage
- external knowledge linkage

### Broken invariant
The usual broken invariant is:

> the output should remain meaningfully tied to the right evidence surface, but it does not

### What F1 typically looks like
Common signs include:

- answers that sound strong but are not sourced properly
- retrieval that looks active but does not actually support the answer
- evidence surfaces that are present but mismatched
- claims that drift away from the cited or retrieved material
- grounding layers that exist in name but not in effect

### What F1 is not
F1 is not simply “the wording is ugly.”  
F1 is not simply “the interface is hard to read.”  
F1 is not simply “the reasoning sounds strange.”

If the core problem is more about bad rendering, packaging, format, or localization, that may be closer to F7.  
If the core problem is more about diagnosis visibility or instrumentation blindness, that may be closer to F5.

### Common family confusions
Most common confusions:
- F1 vs F7
- F1 vs F5

### Light first direction
The first direction is usually:

tighten the evidence surface before trying to decorate the answer.

## F2 · Reasoning and Progression Integrity 🧠

### Core intuition
F2 cases are about the system failing to move through a reasoning path in a stable, coherent, or valid way.

The issue is not mainly that evidence is absent.  
The issue is that the chain of progression is weak, broken, contradictory, or structurally unsound.

### What usually breaks first
The first thing that breaks is usually the path from one step to the next.

That can show up as:

- invalid inference transitions
- broken step linkage
- contradiction within a supposed line of thought
- missing intermediate structure
- jumps that are rhetorically smooth but structurally unsupported

### Broken invariant
The usual broken invariant is:

> the reasoning path should progress coherently from prior support to later conclusion, but it does not

### What F2 typically looks like
Common signs include:

- chain-like answers that skip critical transitions
- step-by-step outputs that are not actually structurally stepwise
- reasoning that wanders or collapses under scrutiny
- conclusions that are not earned by the path that led to them
- internal consistency that looks thinner the closer you inspect it

### What F2 is not
F2 is not simply “the model forgot a previous turn.”  
That may be more about F3.  
F2 is not simply “the text formatting is poor.”  
That may be more about F7.  
F2 is not simply “the system failed to execute the task.”  
That may be more about F4.

### Common family confusions
Most common confusions:
- F2 vs F3
- F2 vs F7

### Light first direction
The first direction is usually:

repair the progression structure before adding more surface detail.

## F3 · State and Continuity Integrity 🧭

### Core intuition
F3 cases are about the system failing to preserve or manage continuity across steps, turns, states, sessions, or evolving task conditions.

The question here is less “can it reason at all?” and more:

can it remain structurally consistent across time and changing state?

### What usually breaks first
The first thing that breaks is usually continuity.

That can involve:

- memory drift
- turn-to-turn inconsistency
- missing carryover state
- forgotten commitments
- state mutation without proper update discipline
- partial context collapse

### Broken invariant
The usual broken invariant is:

> the system should maintain relevant continuity across evolving state, but it does not

### What F3 typically looks like
Common signs include:

- a system that starts correctly and then loses the thread
- earlier commitments being dropped without explanation
- stateful tasks degrading after several steps
- context being partially remembered and partially broken
- prior outputs not being integrated into later outputs coherently

### What F3 is not
F3 is not mainly about invalid reasoning steps by themselves.  
That leans toward F2.  
F3 is not mainly about failure to perform the requested action.  
That leans toward F4.  
F3 is not mainly about observability and instrumentation blindness.  
That leans toward F5.

### Common family confusions
Most common confusions:
- F3 vs F2
- F3 vs F4
- F3 vs F6

### Light first direction
The first direction is usually:

stabilize the continuity surface before adding more task pressure.

## F4 · Execution and Contract Integrity ⚙️

### Core intuition
F4 cases are about the system failing to carry intention into action, procedure, execution, or explicit contract-following behavior.

This family matters when the issue is not just understanding, but doing.

### What usually breaks first
The first thing that breaks is usually reliable action under a known contract.

That can involve:

- failure to follow explicit instructions
- tool misuse
- inability to translate plan into action
- contract drift in execution
- action steps that do not match the task shape
- compliance failure with required output behavior

### Broken invariant
The usual broken invariant is:

> the system should convert task intent and explicit contract into reliable execution, but it does not

### What F4 typically looks like
Common signs include:

- a plan that sounds fine but is not executed correctly
- tools invoked in the wrong order or wrong way
- contract requirements silently ignored
- incomplete execution despite adequate apparent understanding
- task outputs that fail the required shape or action standard

### What F4 is not
F4 is not just “we cannot diagnose what happened.”  
That leans toward F5.  
F4 is not just “the safety boundary is confused.”  
That leans toward F6.  
F4 is not just “the answer wording is badly represented.”  
That leans toward F7.

### Common family confusions
Most common confusions:
- F4 vs F5
- F4 vs F6
- F4 vs F3

### Light first direction
The first direction is usually:

repair the action contract and execution path before adding more abstraction.

## F5 · Observability and Diagnosability Integrity 🔍

### Core intuition
F5 cases are about the system being hard to inspect, hard to diagnose, hard to measure, or hard to read operationally.

Sometimes the core engine may partly work.  
But you cannot see enough of the right signals to understand what is failing, where it is failing, or how to intervene safely.

### What usually breaks first
The first thing that breaks is usually diagnostic visibility.

That can involve:

- blind spots in tracing
- weak instrumentation
- poor metrics
- missing intermediate signals
- inability to distinguish one failure surface from another
- inability to inspect the state of the pipeline meaningfully

### Broken invariant
The usual broken invariant is:

> the system should be diagnosable enough for reliable interpretation and intervention, but it is not

### What F5 typically looks like
Common signs include:

- a system that might be failing in several ways, but the evidence surface is too thin to tell
- outputs that hide the route to failure
- tools or pipelines that run without useful inspection surfaces
- evaluation that exists but is too weak or too indirect to guide repair
- operational blindness that blocks confident classification

### What F5 is not
F5 is not mainly about crossing safety boundaries.  
That leans toward F6.  
F5 is not mainly about missing grounding.  
That may lean toward F1.  
F5 is not mainly about action contract failure.  
That may lean toward F4.

### Common family confusions
Most common confusions:
- F5 vs F6
- F5 vs F1
- F5 vs F4

### Light first direction
The first direction is usually:

increase meaningful visibility before making large structural claims.

## F6 · Boundary and Safety Integrity 🛡️

### Core intuition
F6 cases are about limits, boundaries, constraints, and safety discipline being broken, crossed, or handled unreliably.

This family matters when the system does not respect the right edge conditions.

### What usually breaks first
The first thing that breaks is usually controlled limit behavior.

That can involve:

- missing guardrails
- poor refusal discipline
- unstable constraint handling
- inability to maintain safe boundaries under pressure
- uncontrolled crossing of operational limits
- unclear edge-condition behavior

### Broken invariant
The usual broken invariant is:

> the system should respect and maintain the right operational and safety boundaries, but it does not

### What F6 typically looks like
Common signs include:

- rules that exist but do not hold under pressure
- boundary conditions that collapse in edge cases
- systems that fail to preserve restriction discipline
- inconsistent refusal or constraint behavior
- control surfaces that appear present but do not bite hard enough

### What F6 is not
F6 is not simply “we cannot inspect the failure clearly.”  
That may be F5.  
F6 is not simply “the task was not executed correctly.”  
That may be F4.  
F6 is not simply “the answer was represented badly.”  
That may be F7.

### Common family confusions
Most common confusions:
- F6 vs F5
- F6 vs F4
- F6 vs F3

### Light first direction
The first direction is usually:

restore edge discipline before optimizing throughput, convenience, or style.

## F7 · Representation and Localization Integrity 📝

### Core intuition
F7 cases are about meaning being represented, packaged, rendered, localized, or structured badly at the output or representation surface.

Sometimes the system has part of the right intention underneath.  
But the meaning arrives badly in the final shape.

### What usually breaks first
The first thing that breaks is usually the representation surface.

That can involve:

- poor formatting
- poor localization
- awkward packaging
- misleading rendering
- structural meaning loss at the output surface
- mismatch between internal intent and final expression

### Broken invariant
The usual broken invariant is:

> the system should preserve and render meaning in a usable representational form, but it does not

### What F7 typically looks like
Common signs include:

- answers that may contain some right material but are packaged badly
- localization failures that distort usefulness
- formatting or structure that hides what the system actually means
- representation surfaces that degrade interpretation
- final outputs that confuse users even when some internal signal exists

### What F7 is not
F7 is not mainly about grounding failure.  
That may be F1.  
F7 is not mainly about broken reasoning progression.  
That may be F2.  
F7 is not mainly about execution under contract.  
That may be F4.

### Common family confusions
Most common confusions:
- F7 vs F1
- F7 vs F2

### Light first direction
The first direction is usually:

repair the representation surface without pretending that a surface fix automatically solves deeper structural issues.

## Cross-family comparison notes

The seven families are useful because they separate different kinds of primary failure surface.

That matters because many systems look broken in several ways at once.

For example:

- a grounding failure may later produce representation noise
- a reasoning failure may later look like execution confusion
- an observability failure may block confident separation between several neighboring families
- a boundary failure may look like execution trouble until the edge conditions are examined directly

So the family layer should not be read as a simplistic labeling game.

It should be read as the first disciplined cut.

That first cut is already valuable because it narrows the space of later questions.

---

## Common family-level mistakes

There are several easy mistakes that new readers should avoid.

### Mistake 1. Treating a symptom as if it were already the family
A symptom may be real, but symptoms alone do not always tell you the deepest route-first family.

### Mistake 2. Treating the first repair preference as if it defined the family
A repair move can be helpful without proving the family completely.

### Mistake 3. Treating wording confidence as if it were evidence confidence
A strong tone is not structural support.

### Mistake 4. Forcing fine-grained certainty too early
Sometimes family-level is the right level for now.

### Mistake 5. Forgetting neighboring families
Many real cases sit near a boundary, so the right next move is often to consult the boundary guide rather than claim total closure.

---

## When family-level reading is enough

Family-level reading is often enough when:

- you need the first useful route into the problem
- the primary failure surface is already clear
- the case does not yet need subtree or overlay nuance
- you want to choose the next diagnostic question
- you want to narrow the repair direction without overclaiming certainty

This is one reason the family layer matters so much.

It gives the system a disciplined first landing point.

---

## When family-level reading is not enough

Family-level reading is usually **not** enough when:

- two neighboring families are still both plausible
- the evidence is thin or contradictory
- the case clearly mixes several interacting surfaces
- a subtree-level distinction matters operationally
- a fit registry judgment is needed
- repair risk is high enough that misclassification would be costly

When that happens, the right move is not to stretch this page too far.

The right move is to continue into the next layers:

## Practical use

The easiest practical way to use this page is:

### Step 1
Read the family summary table once.

### Step 2
Find the one or two families that feel closest to the primary failure surface.

### Step 3
Read those family mini-specs, especially:
- broken invariant
- what this family is not
- common confusions

### Step 4
If one family remains clearly stronger, keep that as the current route-first read.

### Step 5
If two families remain live, move to the boundary guide instead of pretending the answer is already settled.

That workflow keeps the Atlas readable and safe.
